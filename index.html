Phase 1 — Foundation · Days 1–20

These 4 tenses cover 80% of everyday English. Master these first.

01
Present Simple
Subject + base verb  (+ s/es for he / she / it)
Days 1–5
›
When to use it
Use Present Simple for:
• Habits & routines — things you do regularly
• General facts — things always true
• Feelings / states — I love, I know, I believe
• Schedules — The bus leaves at 7 am.
every dayalwaysusuallyneveroftensometimeson Mondays
Positive:I/You/We/They work · He/She/It works
Negative:I don't work · She doesn't work
Question:Do you work? · Does she work?
⚠ Add -s/-es for he/she/it:
go → goes  |  watch → watches  |  study → studies
3 clear examples
I drink coffee every morning.
Habit — something I do every day
She works at a hospital.
He/She/It → add -s to the verb
The sun rises in the east.
General fact — always true
More examples:
He doesn't eat meat. (negative)
Does she live near here? (question)
Water boils at 100°C. (fact)
They play football on weekends.
📖 A Day in the Life of Mia
Mia lives in a small city near the coast. She works as a nurse at the local hospital. Every morning, she wakes up at 6 o'clock and drinks a glass of water. Then she makes breakfast — usually eggs and rice.

Mia takes the bus to work because she doesn't have a car. The bus leaves at 7:15 am. At the hospital, she helps patients and speaks to doctors. She loves her job.

After work, Mia usually goes for a walk in the park. She doesn't watch much TV. Instead, she reads books or calls her family. She goes to bed at 10 pm.
Comprehension Questions — answer in full sentences
Where does Mia live and what does she do for work?
What time does she wake up, and what does she eat for breakfast?
How does she get to work? Why doesn't she drive?
What does she do in the evening instead of watching TV?
Part A — Choose the correct answer
She ___ to school every day. (go)
go goes going gone
___ you speak English?
Does Do Are
My brother ___ like coffee. (negative)
don't like doesn't like isn't like
Part B — Write your own sentences
Write a sentence about something you do every morning. Use Present Simple.
Write a sentence about a friend or family member. Use he/she + verb.
02
Past Simple
Subject + verb-ed  (or irregular past form)
Days 6–10
›
When to use it
Use Past Simple for:
• A completed action at a specific time in the past
• A series of past events (She woke up, ate, and left)
• A past habit (I played football as a child)
yesterdaylast nightlast weekin 2020agoin the morning
Regular:work → worked, play → played
Irregular:go → went, eat → ate, see → saw
Negative:I didn't work (same for ALL subjects)
Question:Did you work?  Did she go?
⚠ Common mistake:
Wrong: She didn't went. ✗
Correct: She didn't go. ✓

After didn't/did, always use the BASE form of the verb.
3 clear examples
I watched a movie last night.
Regular verb: watch + ed = watched
She went to the market yesterday.
Irregular: go → went (must memorize!)
They played football on Sunday.
All subjects use the same past form
Key irregular verbs:
have→ had · come→came · take→took
know→ knew · make→made · buy→bought
speak→ spoke · give→gave · see→saw
📖 Kosal's Busy Weekend
Last weekend, Kosal had a very busy schedule. On Saturday morning, he woke up early and went to the market with his mother. They bought fresh vegetables and fruit. Then they came home and cooked lunch together.

In the afternoon, Kosal met his friends at the park. They played football for two hours. After the game, they sat under a tree and talked about their week. Kosal told them a funny story and everyone laughed.

On Sunday, Kosal didn't go out. He stayed home and studied for his English exam. He read his textbook for three hours. At night, he watched an English movie to practise listening.
Comprehension Questions
Where did Kosal go on Saturday morning, and who did he go with?
What did he do with his friends in the afternoon?
Why did he stay home on Sunday?
Find 5 irregular past verbs in the story and write their base forms.
Part A — Choose the correct form

go goed went goes
He ___ breakfast this morning. (negative / eat)
didn't ate didn't eat not ate
___ you watch the game last night?
Do Does Did

03
Present Continuous
Subject + am / is / are + verb-ing
Days 11–15
›
When to use it
Use Present Continuous for:
• Actions happening right now, at this moment
• Temporary situations around now
• Arranged future plans — I am meeting her tomorrow
right nowat the momentcurrentlythis weektodayLook!
I:I am working
He/She/It:She is working
We/You/They:They are working
Negative:I am not working · She isn't working
Question:Are you working? · Is she working?
⚠ Spelling rules for -ing:
swim → swimming (double consonant after short vowel)
make → making (drop silent -e, then add -ing)
study → studying (just add -ing normally)
3 clear examples
I am eating lunch right now.
Happening at this exact moment
She is studying English this week.
Temporary activity around this period
They are building a new house.
Ongoing situation at the moment
Simple vs Continuous:
I drink coffee. → habit, every day ✓
I am drinking coffee. → right now ✓

She works at a hospital. → her permanent job ✓
She is working now. → at this moment ✓
📖 A Busy Afternoon at the Café
It is 3 o'clock in the afternoon. The café is full of people. A woman in the corner is reading a book and is sipping iced coffee. Two students near the window are doing their homework. One of them is writing notes while the other is checking his phone.

Behind the counter, the barista is making a latte. She is listening to music with her headphones. Her colleague is cleaning the tables near the door. A child near the entrance is eating a piece of cake and is laughing at something funny.

Outside, it is raining lightly. People are walking quickly with umbrellas. A man is standing under the café awning, waiting for a taxi. Inside, everyone is staying warm and enjoying the afternoon.
Comprehension Questions
What is the woman in the corner doing?
What are the two students doing near the window?
What is the weather like outside the café?
Challenge: Find and list ALL the Present Continuous verbs in the passage.
Part A — Choose the correct form
Look! The children ___ in the garden right now.
play are playing is playing
I can't talk right now. I ___ dinner.
am cooking cook is cooking
Part B — Writing practice
Look around you right now. Write 2 sentences about what you or someone near you is doing.
04
Future Simple (will)
Subject + will + base verb
Days 16–20
›
When to use it
Use Future Simple (will) for:
• Spontaneous decisions — decided at this moment
• Predictions — I think it will rain tonight
• Promises & offers — I will help you
• Future facts — She will be 30 next year
tomorrownext weeksoonI thinkprobablyin the future
Positive:I/She/They will work (same for ALL subjects)
Short form:I'll · She'll · They'll work
Negative:I will not / won't work
Question:Will you work?  Will she come?
will vs going to:
will → decided RIGHT NOW (spontaneous)
"The phone is ringing — I'll get it!" ✓

going to → planned BEFORE the moment
"I'm going to visit my parents this weekend." ✓
3 clear examples
I will call you tomorrow.
Promise made at this moment
It will rain tonight, I think.
Prediction about the future
She will help you with that.
Spontaneous offer / decision
More examples:
A: There's no milk. B: I'll get some! (spontaneous) ✓
He won't come to the party. (negative) ✓
Will you be at school tomorrow? (question) ✓
I think prices will rise next year. (prediction) ✓
📖 A Letter to My Future Self
Dear Future Me,

I hope you are well. By the time you read this, things will be very different. I believe you will speak English fluently. You will have a good job, and you will live in a comfortable home.

I think technology will change a lot. Cars will be electric. Many people will work from home. I also think that learning apps will be even better — maybe you won't need a teacher at all!

But some things will not change. People will still love their families. They will still eat together, laugh together, and help each other. I think kindness will always be important.

I hope you will be happy. I will work hard now so that your life will be easier. With love, Past You.
Comprehension Questions
What does the writer hope for their future English skills?
What changes does the writer predict about technology and work?
What things does the writer believe will NOT change?
Count all the "will" and "won't" forms in the letter. How many are there?
Part A — Choose the correct answer
A: I'm thirsty. B: I ___ get you some water.
will am going to would
I don't think it ___ rain today. The sky is clear.
isn't going to won't doesn't
Part B — Writing practice
Write 2 predictions about your life in 5 years. Use "I think I will..." and "I probably won't..."

05
Future with "Going to"
Subject + am / is / are + going to + base verb
Days 21–23
›
When to use it
Use "going to" for:
• Plans decided before this moment
• Evidence-based predictions — you can see something is about to happen right now
this weekendnext monthsoonLook!
I:I am going to travel
He/She:She is going to study
We/They:They are going to move
3 clear examples
I am going to visit my parents this weekend.
Already planned before this conversation
Look at those clouds — it is going to rain.
You can see the evidence right now
We are going to move to a new apartment.
Decision made before speaking
📖 Srey's Big Plans
Srey is going to have a very busy month. Next week, she is going to start a new English course at a language school downtown. She is going to attend classes three times a week in the evenings.

Her family is going to travel to the province next weekend to visit their grandparents. They are going to stay there for three days. Her brother is going to drive the car because their father is not feeling well.

At work, Srey is going to give a presentation next Friday. She is going to prepare the slides this week. Her manager told her the company is going to hire two new staff members next month, which means Srey is going to be less busy soon.
Comprehension Questions
What course is Srey going to start, and how often will she attend?
Where is the family going to travel, and why is her father not driving?
What is Srey going to do at work next Friday?
Part A — Quick quiz

is going to will falls
Part B — Writing practice
Write 3 sentences about your real plans for next week using "going to".

When to use it
An action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past. Often pairs with Past Simple:

whenwhileat 8 pmat that time
I/He/She/It:I was working
We/You/They:They were working

Question:Were you sleeping?

📖 The Power Cut
Last Tuesday evening, the electricity suddenly went out across the neighbourhood. At that moment, many people were doing different things. In house number 12, a family was eating dinner when all the lights went off. The mother was cutting vegetables and the children were laughing at something on TV.

Next door, a student was studying for an important exam when his laptop screen went dark. He wasn't sleeping — he was reading his notes very carefully. Across the street, two neighbours were talking outside when they heard a loud sound from the power station.

It took two hours for the power to return. While everyone was waiting in the dark, they found candles and told stories to each other. It was actually a lovely evening in the end.
Comprehension Questions
What was the family in house 12 doing when the lights went off?
Was the student sleeping? What was he actually doing?
What did people do while they were waiting for the power to return?
Part A — Choose the correct form

took was taking were taking
Part B — Writing practice

07
Present Perfect
Subject + have / has + past participle
Days 28–35
›
When to use it
Use Present Perfect for:
• Life experiences — no specific time mentioned
• Recent past with a present result
• Actions starting in the past and continuing now
alreadyjustyeteverneverbeforesincefor
I/You/We:I have visited (I've visited)
He/She/It:She has finished (She's finished)
Negative:I haven't eaten · She hasn't come
Question:Have you ever been there?
Perfect vs Past Simple:
I have visited Bangkok. (experience, no time given) ✓
I visited Bangkok in 2019. (specific time) ✓

She has just eaten. (result: she's full now) ✓
She ate an hour ago. (finished, no connection to now) ✓
3 clear examples
I have visited Bangkok before.
Life experience — we don't say when
She has just finished her homework.
"just" = very recently, result = she's free now
They have never eaten sushi.
"never" = zero times in their whole life
📖 A World Traveller's Story
My uncle is an adventurer. In his life, he has visited more than 40 countries. He has climbed mountains in Nepal, has swum in the Great Barrier Reef, and has eaten foods that most people have never tried.

He has lived in three different countries — Thailand, Spain, and Canada. He has learned four languages. I have always admired him because he has never been afraid to try new things.

This year, he has already been to Japan and Vietnam. He has just returned from a trip to Mongolia. He told me that he hasn't visited South America yet — but he has already planned a trip there for next year.
Comprehension Questions
How many countries has the uncle visited in his life?
Which three countries has he lived in?
Which continent hasn't he been to yet?
Find 3 examples of "just/already/yet/never/ever" in the passage.
Part A — Choose the correct answer
I ___ to Japan. It was amazing! (no specific time)
went have been has been
She ___ her keys. She can't open the door now.
lost have lost has lost
Part B — Writing practice

08
Present Perfect Continuous
Subject + have / has been + verb-ing
Days 36–39
›
When to use it
An action that started in the past and is still happening now. The focus is on the duration — how long it has been happening.
for 2 hourssince morningall daylatelyrecently
I/You/We:I have been studying
He/She/It:She has been working
Perfect vs Perfect Continuous:
I have read 3 chapters. → result, how many ✓
I have been reading for 2 hours. → duration, how long ✓
3 clear examples
I have been studying English for 2 months.
Started 2 months ago, still continuing now
She has been working here since 2020.
"since" = from a specific point in time
It has been raining all day.
Started this morning, still raining now
Quick quiz
He looks tired. He ___ all night. (study)
studied has been studying have been studying

09
Past Perfect
Subject + had + past participle
Days 40–43
›
When to use it
An action completed BEFORE another past action. Use it to show which event happened first when telling a past story.
alreadybeforeby the timeafterwhen
All subjects:I / She / They had finished
Negative:She hadn't eaten
Question:Had you seen it before?
Think of it as a timeline:
← Earlier                 Later →
had left   →   I arrived

When I arrived, she had already left.
(she left FIRST, then I arrived SECOND)
3 clear examples
When I arrived, she had already left.
She left FIRST → I arrived SECOND
He ate a lot because he hadn't eaten all day.
Not eating was earlier; eating was later
I had never seen snow before I went to Korea.
Zero experiences before a past moment in time
Quick quiz
By the time the film started, we ___ our seats. (find)
found had found have found
Writing practice
Write a sentence using "When I arrived / got home, I had already..."

12
Past & Future Perfect Continuous
had been + ing  ·  will have been + ing
Days 51–60
›
When to use it
Past Perfect Continuous:
Duration of an action that was ongoing before a past moment.

Future Perfect Continuous:
Duration of an action that will be ongoing until a future point.
Past Perf. C:I had been waiting for 2 hours
Future Perf. C:I will have been studying for 6 months
These tenses are rare in everyday conversation. Don't worry if they feel difficult — focus on mastering the simpler tenses first and you will naturally use these as you advance.
2 clear examples
She had been waiting for 2 hours before he arrived.
Duration of waiting before a past event
By June, I will have been studying English for 6 months.
Duration of studying up to a future point
